  • "Language is important: some words hurt and are best avoided. But that hurt is culturally contextual, not universal: words that are harmful within one context may be entirely benign in another (Butler 1993)" (Jas 2020: 81)
  • There exist "different languages of gender and sexuality in both 'Western' (e.g. Valentine 2007) and non-'Western' cultures (e.g. Jackson 1989; Kulick 1998) [...] our conceptual frameworks and resulting language are rarely if ever one-size-fits-all" (Jas 2020: 81)
  • Throw out existing (Anglospheric) language and look at what lies beneath

Identity

  • Identity is self-described
    • "[A]nyone can be anything and identify any way they like (with regard to gender)" (Jas 2020: 81)
    • Logically, identity shouldn't be a factor in attraction
  • However, might character or personality matter?
  • Could identity actually play a role in fetishes? Do fetishes even fall within the domain of attraction and orientation?

Aesthetics

  • Aesthetics are usually the first thing you see on a dating app, and attraction at first sight - with no further knowledge - is entirely possible
  • Where do you draw the line between aesthetics and morphology?

Morphology

  • Common to think of gender and morphology as aligned, which leads to viewing 'lesbian', 'gay' and 'straight' as attraction to particular types of bodies
  • Trans people complicate this - an attraction to a particular type of body does not equal an attraction to a particular gender
  • Are these categories only ostensibly gender-based, and actually about morphology?
  • Some trans people view claims of morphological preference as biologically essentialist and problematic, e.g. Kay below
